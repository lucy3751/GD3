1. Different types of rice

There are more than 40,000 varieties of rice in the world. But they can be broken down into three classifications based on their size - long-grain, medium-grain, and short-grain.

Since rice is composed of starch, all rice are high in carbohydrates. Unlike the other colored grains, the bran is removed in white rice grains, hence there are more starch and higher carbohydrates.
Furthermore, sticky and sushi rice (both short-grain, white color) are high in both sodium and sugar. When cooked, they become more sticky. They also have a high glycaemic index, which causes the Carbohydrates to break down quickly, causing a rapid increase in blood-sugar levels.
The bran contains most of the fiber. Since the bran isn't removed in the brown, wild, and red rice, they are rich in fiber compared to the white colored grains.
Surprisingly, wild rice is the only grain that contains Vitamin A. It also has more potassium and protein compared to the other grains.

3. How is rice produced?

Rice actually comes from Oryza plants. Since there are many different types of rice, the types of rice plants are diverse as well. The production methods of rice varies in different countries. But they can be classified into three different methods: irrigation, rainfed lowland, and rainfed upland.
For the irrigation method, the rice paddies are flooded. Since rice requires a lot of water to grow, this method is yields the most rice. Hence it is the most popular method, occupying 54% of the global rice harvested area, and responsible for 75% of the global rice population. 40% of the global irrigation water and 30% of the freshwater resources are used to grow irrigation rice.
For the rainfed lowland method, rice are grown near rivers so that it can be flooded with rainwater. However, due to occasional drought periods, it doesn't produce that much rice. This method occupies 30% of the global rice harvested area and is responsible for 20% of the global rice population.
For the rainfed upland method, rice is grown without irrigation and flooding. It is similar to growing wheat. Since the soil conditions are very unstable (sometimes highly fertile to infertile), it doesn't yield that much rice as well. Hence this method occupies only 7% of the global rice harvested area and is responsible for only 1% of the global rice population.

4. Which country produces rice?

Despite being the top producer, China is also the top importer of rice. This may be due to the huge population and rice being the staple food. In 2020/2021, China is the top country in rice consumption. We can see similar situation with India as well, which is the second in rice consumption.

5. Rice produces methane

Since it is a global staple food, rice is the crop that produces the most methane .
Because rice is grown by flooding the rice fields/paddies, the water blocks the soil from oxygen, which creates microorganisms in the soil that produces methane. The longer the rice paddies are being flooded, the more microorganisms are created, and more methane is produced.

Since the irrigation method is the most productive and involves flooding the rice for most of the time, countries (like China and India) that use this method would rank higher in both rice and methane production. While countries like Africa which mostly uses upland and lowland methods, would produces less rice and methane.
With the global population increasing, the global rice consumption would surely increase as well. Sustainable methods of rice production must be implemented to protect the environment. In 2019, the Chinese government and the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation funded the Green Super Rice (GSR) project, which aims to create rice varieties that can grow in the harsh environment, therefore increasing rice production in countries that uses the rainfed lowland and upland methods.
